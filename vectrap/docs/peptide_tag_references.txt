[1] His_tag
    Sequence (regex):  H{6,9}
    Canonical example: HHHHHH  (6x), HHHHHHHHH (9x)
    Size:              6-9 AA
    Description:
        Polyhistidine tag; the most widely used affinity tag in recombinant
        protein production. Binds immobilized metal ions (Ni2+, Co2+) via
        chelation. Functions when appended to either terminus and in native
        or denaturing conditions. The 6xHis variant is canonical; longer
        runs (7-9) are also common and captured by this entry.
    Reference:
        Hochuli E, Bannwarth W, Dobeli H, Gentz R, Stuber D.
        "Genetic approach to facilitate purification of recombinant proteins
        with a novel metal chelate adsorbent."
        Biotechnology (1988) 6:1321-1325.
        DOI: 10.1038/nbt1188-1321

    Description:
        Extended polyhistidine run of 10 or more residues. Provides
        significantly higher binding capacity and affinity to Ni-NTA resin
        compared to 6xHis, enabling purification under more stringent
        wash conditions and from crude lysates with high imidazole.
        Detected separately from the 6-9x tier to distinguish constructs.
    Reference:
        Bornhorst JA, Falke JJ.
        "Purification of proteins using polyhistidine affinity tags."
        Methods Enzymol (2000) 326:245-254.
        DOI: 10.1016/s0076-6879(00)26058-8

[22] TEV_cleavage

    Description:
        Recognition site for Tobacco Etch Virus (TEV) NIa protease.

[27] Flexible_linker_GGS
    Sequence (regex):  (?:G{3,4}S){2,}
    Canonical example: GGGSGGGS  (2x GGGS) or GGGGSGGGS (mixed)
    Description:
        Glycine-serine flexible linker. G residues confer conformational
        flexibility; S residues maintain solubility. Two or more GGGS/
        GGGGS units constitute a true designed linker rather than an
        accidental run. Used between domains in fusion proteins and
        between tag and target to provide spatial separation.
    Reference:
        Chen X, Zaro JL, Shen WC.
        "Fusion protein linkers: property, design and functionality."
        Adv Drug Deliv Rev (2013) 65:1357-1369.
        DOI: 10.1016/j.addr.2012.09.039

[28] Rigid_linker_EAAAK
    Sequence (regex):  (?:EAAAK){2,}
    Canonical example: EAAAKEAAAK (2x)
    Description:
        Alpha-helical rigid linker based on repeating EAAAK units.
        Forms a stable alpha-helix due to salt bridges between E and K
        across adjacent turns. Provides fixed spatial separation between
        domains, preferred when functional independence of two fused
        domains is required. Two or more repeats constitute a true linker.
    Reference:
        Arai R, Ueda H, Kitayama A, Kamiya N, Nagamune T.
        "Design of the linkers which effectively separate domains of a
        bifunctional fusion protein."
        Protein Eng (2001) 14:529-532.
        DOI: 10.1093/protein/14.8.529

[29] Rigid_linker_PAPAP
    Sequence (regex):  (?:PAPAP){2,}
    Canonical example: PAPAPPAPAP (2x, with overlap possible)
    Description:
        Proline-alanine alternating rigid linker. Prolines strongly
        disfavor alpha-helix and beta-sheet, forcing the linker into
        an extended polyproline II-like conformation. Provides
        predictable, rod-like separation between domains. Used in
        antibody engineering and bi-specific construct design.
    Reference:
        Klein JS, Jiang S, Galimidi RP, Keeffe JR, Bjorkman PJ.
        "Design and characterization of structured protein linkers
        with differing flexibilities."
        Protein Eng Des Sel (2014) 27:325-330.
        DOI: 10.1093/protein/gzu043

[30] Helical_linker_A4
    Sequence (regex):  (?:AAAA){3,}
    Canonical example: AAAAAAAAAAAA (3x AAAA = 12 AA)
    Description:
        Poly-alanine alpha-helical linker. Alanine has the highest
        alpha-helix propensity of any amino acid, making long alanine
        runs rigidly helical. Three or more AAAA repeats (12+ AA)
        form a stable helix in solution. Used in synthetic biology
        constructs to provide defined helical spacers. Note: long
        alanine runs can also arise from low-complexity regions;
        hits should be evaluated in genomic context.
    Reference:
        Marqusee S, Robbins VH, Baldwin RL.
        "Unusually stable helix formation in short alanine-based
        peptides."
        PNAS (1989) 86:5286-5290.
        DOI: 10.1073/pnas.86.14.5286
